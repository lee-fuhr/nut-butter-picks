Nut Butters questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Natural peanut butter and almond butter are minimally processed spreads made from just nuts and maybe salt, unlike conventional peanut butter which adds sugar, hydrogenated oils, and emulsifiers. The key buying advice: look for a label with one ingredient (the nut) plus optional salt, expect to stir the separated oil, and store it in the fridge after opening to extend freshness. Both offer healthy fats and protein, but almond butter has more vitamin E and a slightly different flavor profile; choose based on taste and dietary needs.

Natural peanut butter vs. regular: what’s the difference?

Natural peanut butter contains just peanuts, sometimes a pinch of salt, while regular peanut butter adds sugar, hydrogenated vegetable oil, and emulsifiers to prevent separation and give a creamy, spreadable texture. The hydrogenated oils in conventional brands introduce trans fats, even if labeled “zero grams” per serving, and the added sugar can be significant. From a taste perspective, natural peanut butter has a roasty, pure peanut flavor that many find superior, but it requires stirring because the oil rises to the top. Regular peanut butter stays uniform on the shelf but tastes sweeter and more processed. If you’re prioritizing whole ingredients and avoiding additives, natural is the clear choice.

How to choose a good almond butter?

Read the label first. The best almond butters list “almonds” as the only ingredient, sometimes with sea salt. Avoid added sugars (cane sugar, honey, maple syrup), palm oil, and “natural flavors” that dilute the pure almond taste. Some brands offer “stir” varieties with a bit of palm oil to reduce separation, but purists prefer single-ingredient versions. Texture is a personal call: crunchy (with almond pieces) versus creamy. Creamy almond butter is often drier than peanut butter and can stick to the roof of your mouth or seem gritty if the almonds are not ground long enough. Smaller-batch brands sometimes produce a smoother, more buttery consistency, while large-scale ones may be more prone to graininess.

Is natural nut butter healthier than regular?

Yes, primarily because natural nut butters contain no hydrogenated oils, no added sugars (unless you choose a sweetened variety), and no preservatives. Both natural and regular nut butters are calorie-dense (around 190–210 calories per two tablespoons) and rich in unsaturated fats, protein, and fiber, but the quality of those fats is better in natural versions. Regular peanut butter often has 1–2 grams of added sugar per serving and may use hydrogenated oils to stabilize the spread, which contribute unhealthy trans fats. For almond butter, many commercial versions add sugar and palm oil, turning a nutrient-dense food into a dessert spread. Read the label to ensure you’re getting the full nutritional benefits of the nuts themselves.

How to store nut butters to keep them fresh?

Natural nut butters should be refrigerated after opening to slow oxidation and prevent rancidity. The cold solidifies the oil a bit, making stirring a little harder, but the freshness payoff is huge. Unopened jars can sit in a cool, dark pantry for several months. Once opened, stir thoroughly before first use, then refrigerate. The texture will be firmer when cold; let the jar sit at room temperature for a few minutes to soften if you prefer a spreadable consistency. Avoid double-dipping with a knife that has crumbs or other foods to prevent contamination.

What should I look for on the label?

Start with the ingredient list. For any nut butter, you want one ingredient: the nut. Salt is acceptable, but it should be listed separately. Avoid any form of added sugar (cane sugar, brown rice syrup, agave, honey), hydrogenated or partially hydrogenated oils, and “natural flavors” that can hide additives. Check the fat content per serving: natural nut butters have around 16–18 grams of fat, mostly monounsaturated. Also look at fiber and protein, both should be at least 3 grams per serving for peanut butter and 1–2 grams for almond butter (almonds have less protein by weight). If you see palm oil, the butter may be more spreadable but less pure.

Are there nut butters for special diets?

Most natural nut butters are vegan and gluten-free, but always verify the label for cross-contamination risks if you have celiac disease. For lower-carb or keto diets, almond butter is popular because it has fewer net carbs per serving than peanut butter (around 3 grams vs. 4–5 grams). Some brands add flax or chia seeds to increase fiber and omega-3s, which can fit a keto or paleo template. For nut allergies, sunflower seed butter (made from sunflower seeds) is a common alternative, though it has a distinct grassy flavor and can turn green when baked due to a natural reaction with baking soda. Soy nut butter and tahini (sesame seed butter) are other options, each with different taste and nutrition profiles.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to refrigerate natural peanut butter?

Yes, after opening, refrigeration slows oil separation and prevents the fats from going rancid. The butter will be firmer when cold, but you can let it sit at room temperature for a few minutes to soften for spreading.

Why does oil separate in natural nut butter?

Because natural nut butters contain no emulsifiers or hydrogenated oils, the oil naturally rises to the top over time. This is a sign of a pure product; simply stir the oil back in with a butter knife or fork before each use.

Can I use almond butter as a substitute for peanut butter in recipes?

Yes, but expect a texture and flavor difference. Almond butter is typically less sweet and can be grainier; you may need to add a pinch of salt or adjust moisture in baked goods. It works well in sauces, smoothies, and energy bars.

What is the best nut butter for people with nut allergies?

Sunflower seed butter is the most common nut-free alternative, but it has a distinct flavor and may turn green when combined with baking soda. Tahini (sesame seed paste) and soy nut butter are other options; always check for cross-contamination warnings on the label.

How long does natural nut butter last after opening?

If refrigerated, natural nut butter stays fresh for 3 to 6 months. If kept in the pantry, the shelf life drops to about 1–2 months before the oil begins to go rancid. Trust your nose, if it smells bitter or like old paint, discard it.

Why is some almond butter very sticky and hard to spread?

Almonds naturally have less oil than peanuts, so freshly ground almond butter can be thick and sticky, especially if no oil has been added. Chilling it makes it even stiffer. To improve spreadability, let it come to room temperature or stir in a teaspoon of neutral oil (like avocado oil) before using.

In shortThe best nut butter is the one you’ll actually eat, but by choosing minimally processed versions with just nuts and maybe salt, you get all the flavor and nutrition without the additives. Embrace the routine of stirring and refrigerating, it’s a small step that pays off in taste and health. Whether you reach for peanut butter, almond butter, or a seed-based alternative, read the label, keep it fresh, and enjoy your spread with confidence.
